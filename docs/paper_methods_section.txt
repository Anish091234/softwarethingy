2.X  Radiation Shielding Analysis: LunaRad

2.X.1  Software Purpose and Scope

Radiation shielding performance of candidate wall configurations was
evaluated using LunaRad, a conceptual analysis tool developed to
provide rapid comparative estimates of radiation dose reduction behind
multi-layer habitat walls. The tool is designed for the early design phase,
where numerous material configurations and geometric arrangements must be
screened before committing to computationally expensive Monte Carlo or
deterministic transport simulations. LunaRad implements an
OLTARIS-inspired workflow [Singleterry et al., 2011] consisting of
environment specification, geometry definition, directional ray casting,
and dose metric computation. It is not a particle transport code; rather,
it employs empirically parameterized dose-depth response functions derived
from published HZETRN [Wilson et al., 1995; Wilson et al., 2006] and Monte
Carlo [Slaba et al., 2011] calculations. All results are classified as
conceptual estimates with documented uncertainty bounds.

2.X.2  Radiation Environment Models

The galactic cosmic ray (GCR) environment was modeled using the
Badhwar-O'Neill 2014 (BO2014) parameterization [O'Neill et al., 2015],
which provides differential flux spectra for ions from hydrogen (Z = 1)
through iron (Z = 26) as a function of a single solar modulation parameter.
Calculations were performed at solar minimum conditions (phi = 400 MV),
representing the highest GCR flux and thus the most conservative shielding
requirement for chronic exposure assessment. Solar particle event (SPE)
environments were represented using Band-function spectral fits [Band et
al., 1993; Tylka et al., 2006] to the October 1989 event, historically one
of the largest recorded SPE sequences and widely used as a design reference
event [NCRP, 2006]. The unshielded free-space radiation environment at 1 AU
was assumed, with no magnetospheric shielding applied, consistent with the
lunar surface environment where only minimal solid-body shielding from the
Moon itself is available (reducing the isotropic flux by approximately half
for a surface habitat).

2.X.3  Geometry Representation

The habitat wall was modeled as a multi-layer slab comprising, from outside
to inside: a sintered regolith structural layer, an optional regolith fill
layer, and a PEEK (polyether ether ketone) composite inner structural
shell. Each layer was defined by its physical thickness (cm), bulk density
(g/cm^3), and material composition. Directional ray casting from interior
dosimetry points through the wall stack was performed using analytic
ray-shell intersection for the cylindrical habitat geometry. A total of 768
uniformly distributed ray directions (HEALPix N_side = 8) were used per
dosimetry point, and material-specific path lengths were computed for each
ray through each wall layer. Path lengths were converted to areal densities
(g/cm^2) by multiplication with layer densities.

2.X.4  Shielding Analysis Method

For each ray direction, the total areal density traversed was used to
evaluate empirically parameterized dose-depth response functions of the
form f(x) = A exp(-alpha x) + B exp(-beta x) + C, where x is the areal
density in g/cm^2 and the parameters {A, B, alpha, beta, C} are
material-specific coefficients fit to published dose-depth curves. The first
exponential term captures primary particle attenuation, while the second
exponential and constant terms approximate secondary particle buildup
approaching equilibrium at depth. Parameters for aluminum and polyethylene
were fit to HZETRN results from Wilson et al. [1995] and Cucinotta et al.
[2006]. Parameters for lunar regolith were derived from the experimental
measurements of Miller et al. [2009] using JSC-1A lunar simulant and from
the transport calculations of Singleterry et al. [2013]. Parameters for
PEEK were estimated by effective-Z scaling from polyethylene parameters,
validated against the limited available data for polymer-matrix composites.
Radiation quality factors were applied following ICRP Publication 103
[ICRP, 2007] to convert absorbed dose to dose equivalent. The directional
dose contributions were averaged over all ray directions, weighted by solid
angle, to produce omnidirectional dose estimates.

2.X.5  Output Quantities

The analysis produced the following quantities for each wall configuration:
annual GCR dose equivalent rate (mSv/yr) and event-integrated SPE dose
equivalent (mSv) behind the shielding, dose reduction factors relative to
the unshielded environment, and effective quality factors. These quantities
allow direct comparison of candidate wall configurations against NASA
permissible exposure limits [NCRP, 2000] on a relative basis.

2.X.6  Validation Approach

Tool outputs were compared against published dose-depth data for aluminum
shielding from NCRP Report 153 [NCRP, 2006], Cucinotta et al. [2006], and
HZETRN results from Wilson et al. [1995] at areal densities of 5, 10, 20,
and 50 g/cm^2. Agreement within 20-30% for GCR dose equivalent was
confirmed across this range, consistent with the tool's intended use for
comparative design screening rather than absolute dose certification.
Trend validation confirmed monotonic dose decrease with increasing areal
density, correct ranking of material effectiveness, and proper solar-cycle
dependence. Regression tests ensure reproducibility across code versions.

2.X.7  Key Limitations

The following limitations are acknowledged and documented within the tool
output. Shielding transport is approximated using one-dimensional
exponential attenuation along each ray with no lateral particle transport.
Electromagnetic cascades are not simulated. Secondary neutron buildup is
treated with empirical factors rather than explicit neutron transport.
Nuclear fragmentation is captured through empirical correction factors
rather than fragment-by-fragment tracking. The method is validated for
areal densities of 0-100 g/cm^2 and the standard GCR/SPE environments
described above. For configurations identified as promising by this
screening analysis, higher-fidelity validation using PHITS or HZETRN is
recommended and is supported by the tool's modular solver interface.
